# Charlie Kirk Files Forced Disclosure Project

## What This Project Is

This repository is a legislative drafting and public disclosure project created by Bryan Starbuck. Its purpose is to draft four federal laws that would force full government disclosure and investigation into the death of Charlie Kirk, who was killed on September 10, 2025, at Utah Valley University (UVU) in Orem, Utah. The case is *State of Utah v. Tyler Alexander Robinson*, Case No. 251403576, Fourth Judicial District Court, Utah County.

The project is modeled after Congressman Thomas Massie's Epstein Files Transparency Act (Public Law 119-38), which forced the Department of Justice to release files related to the Jeffrey Epstein investigation. That law passed the House 427-1 and the Senate by unanimous consent. This project seeks to create a parallel set of laws -- but with stronger enforcement mechanisms, because even the Epstein Act suffered compliance failures (DOJ released only approximately 12,285 documents out of potentially 6+ million pages by the statutory deadline).

The core thesis of the project is that citizen investigators have identified extensive evidence suggesting intelligence-service-level involvement in the Charlie Kirk assassination, and that federal agencies are withholding critical information and failing to investigate key leads. The project identifies 175 specific disclosure items organized across categories including foreign intelligence activity, FBI conduct, electronic warfare, financial connections, evidence chain-of-custody issues, and more.

## The Four Laws

The project is organized into four directories, each containing a proposed federal law:

### Law 1: DoJ/FBI Forced Disclosure Act (`1_DoJ_FBI/`)
Forces the Department of Justice, FBI, ATF, CBP, TSA, DHS, FinCEN, and all federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies to disclose all files related to the Charlie Kirk investigation within 30 days of enactment. Uses a dual-track approach: a broad catch-all mandate requiring disclosure of ALL related information, plus specific enumeration of the 175 items in Schedule A. Key features include criminal penalties (10 years for withholding, 15 years for destruction), no presidential override, a permanent congressional review board, automatic 25% budget reductions for non-compliance, private right of action for citizens, whistleblower protections, records preservation triggered at bill introduction, and an anti-embarrassment clause preventing withholding based on reputational harm.

### Law 2: Intelligence Services Disclosure Act (`2_US_Intel/`)
Forces all U.S. Intelligence Community agencies (CIA, NSA, DIA, NRO, NGA, military intelligence, FBI intelligence division, DHS intelligence, ODNI, and others as defined in 50 U.S.C. 3003(4)) to release all information that could identify perpetrators or provide insight into the death. Permits redaction only of currently active human intelligence source identities and specific technical collection methods currently in active use -- but the analytical conclusions and final output information must always be disclosed. Includes the same enforcement mechanisms as Law 1, adapted for the intelligence community's distinct legal framework around classification and sources and methods.

### Law 3: Mandatory Investigation Act (`3_Require_to_Investigate/`)
Requires the FBI and intelligence community to actually conduct a thorough, good-faith investigation -- not merely disclose existing records. Mandates a dedicated FBI team of at least 20 agents with a $10 million independent budget, led by a senior agent with no prior case involvement. Requires investigation of every one of the 175 Schedule A items, with specific categories enumerated (exploding microphone theory, foreign intelligence involvement, evidence of cover-up, alternative shooter theories, financial connections, psychological operations, pre-planning/foreknowledge, electronic warfare). Establishes an independent external monitor appointed by Congress with authority to direct investigators to pursue neglected leads. Includes a conflict-of-interest trigger: if evidence emerges that a covered agency was itself involved, investigation authority transfers to an independent congressional commission.

### Law 4: Trusted Investigators Act (`4_Trusted_Investigations/`)
Establishes independent investigation teams led by three Designated Trusted Investigators: Candace Owens, Dave Smith, and Tucker Carlson. Each gets one FBI team and one Intelligence Community team (six teams total). The trusted investigators have sole authority to select team members, full access to all agency files and systems, authority to compel document production and conduct forensic audits, and the power to publicly release findings. Government employees may volunteer for these teams confidentially, bypassing their chain of command. Non-interference is enforced with up to 15 years imprisonment. Teams operate for a minimum of 24 months, cannot be terminated by any government official, and produce public reports every 90 days addressing each of the 175 Schedule A items.

## Legal Writing Standards for the Four Laws

When modifying any of the four law files, apply the following standards:

### Format and Structure
- Each law is written as a proposed federal statute in markdown format. The markdown format with hyperlinks is acceptable -- it does not need to conform to GPO typesetting standards. The substantive legal provisions must be written at the quality level of legislation ready for introduction in Congress.
- Use standard federal legislative structure: Title, Purpose, Definitions, substantive sections, enforcement, effective date.
- Definitions must be precise and comprehensive. Use established U.S. Code cross-references where applicable (e.g., 50 U.S.C. 3003(4) for Intelligence Community definition).
- Each section should be internally complete -- a reader should understand the mandate, the covered parties, the enforcement mechanism, and the penalties without needing to cross-reference other sections.

### Substantive Legal Standards
- **Mandatory language**: Use "shall" for obligations, never "should" or "may" when imposing duties. "May" is appropriate only for permissions and authorizations.
- **Enforcement teeth**: Every mandate must have a specific enforcement mechanism. Learn from the Epstein Act and JFK Records Act failures -- laws without real penalties become suggestions. Include criminal penalties with specific imprisonment terms, automatic budget reductions, contempt of Congress proceedings, and private rights of action.
- **Anti-evasion provisions**: Agencies routinely exploit ambiguity to withhold records. Include catch-all provisions, anti-embarrassment clauses, prohibitions on reclassification to avoid disclosure, and requirements that specific enumeration does not limit broad mandates.
- **No presidential override**: Based on the JFK Records Act failure where successive presidents continued withholding records for 30+ years, these laws explicitly prohibit presidential suspension or modification.
- **Whistleblower protections**: Must include criminal penalties for retaliation, rebuttable presumption of retaliation for adverse actions within 2 years, mandatory reinstatement, and treble damages.
- **Records preservation**: Triggered at bill introduction (not enactment) to prevent pre-passage destruction. Include forensic audit requirements.

### Scope
- Never limit geographic scope to Utah. All provisions apply across the United States and internationally where U.S. agencies have jurisdiction or possession of records.
- "People involved" is defined comprehensively in the 35 categories (a through ii) in other/people.md. Laws should reference or incorporate this definition.
- Schedule A (the 175 items) is incorporated by reference from the GitHub repository and is also included directly as an appendix in the law text.

### Cross-References Between Laws
- Law 1 (law enforcement disclosure) and Law 2 (intelligence disclosure) are companion statutes covering different agency types.
- Law 3 (mandatory investigation) goes beyond disclosure to require active investigation of all 175 items.
- Law 4 (trusted investigators) provides independent civilian oversight with embedded teams.
- Each law should cross-reference the others where appropriate, particularly for consistent definitions, whistleblower protections, and Schedule A references.

### Precedent and Legal Research
- Primary precedent: Epstein Files Transparency Act (Public Law 119-38) -- the structural model.
- Secondary precedent: JFK Assassination Records Collection Act of 1992 (Public Law 102-526) -- primarily a cautionary example of what fails.
- Brady v. Maryland (373 U.S. 83, 1963) -- establishes the prosecution's duty to disclose exculpatory evidence. The project seeks to extend Brady obligations to FBI and intelligence agencies as custodial parties.
- The notes files in each law's `more/` directory contain detailed legal research and analysis.
