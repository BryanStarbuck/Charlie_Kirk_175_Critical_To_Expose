# Problems, Vulnerabilities, and Recommended Fixes — REMAINING UNFIXED ITEMS

**Analyzed by:** Attorney review of all four laws against the primary charter (`other/List_Of_Laws.md`)
**Date:** March 11, 2026
**Updated:** March 18, 2026 — Fixed items moved to `Fixed/Any_Problems.md`. 5 partially-fixed items moved back from Fixed on March 18. Items below remain unfixed or partially fixed.
**Method:** Threat analysis from the perspective of those who want to keep the investigation covered up.

---

====================

## LAW 1: The Charlie Kirk Files Forced Disclosure Act -- Law Enforcement

**File:** `1_DoJ_FBI/Law_1_DoJ_FBI.md`

### 1. Budget Reduction Penalty Is 60% Weaker Than Charter Requires

**Problem:** The charter specifies "25% automatic budget reduction per month of non-compliance." Law 1 implements only 10% per month with a 50% cap (Section 7(b)). At 10%/month with a 50% cap, an agency can stall for 5 months before hitting maximum penalty, and even then retains 50% of its budget.

**What remains:** Section 7(b) still says 10% per month with 50% cap. Needs to be changed to 25% per month with no cap, with reductions automatic and self-executing upon review board certification.

---

### 2. Private Right of Action 60-Day Notice Period Creates a Destruction Window

**Problem:** Section 9(a) requires 60 days' written notice before citizens can sue. This creates a 60-day window during which agencies can destroy evidence after being put on notice of a specific compliance failure.

**What remains:** The 60-day notice period has not been reduced. Needs reduction to 14 days with an emergency injunction provision allowing immediate filing when evidence destruction is imminent.

---

### 3. State and Local Cooperation Penalties Are Toothless

**Problem:** Section 12(a) limits grant suspension to 10% of Byrne JAG and COPS grants. This is a minor financial consequence for state/local agencies that may be actively covering up.

**What remains:** No escalation to 25% of ALL federal funding. No individual criminal liability for state/local officials who obstruct disclosure. The 10% of two specific grants is insufficient deterrent.

---

### 4. Review Board Is Appointed by Congressional Leadership Who May Want a Cover-Up

**Problem:** Section 8(a) has all five review board members appointed by congressional leadership (Speaker, Majority Leader, Minority Leader). If leadership wants to suppress the investigation, they appoint compliant members.

**What remains:** No appointments by independent authorities (e.g., Chief Justice, GAO Comptroller General). The review board remains entirely under congressional leadership control.

---

### 5. No Mandatory Forensic Audit Before Agencies Certify Compliance — PARTIALLY FIXED

**What's fixed:** Section 5(c) requires agencies to "conduct a digital forensic audit" and Section 12H provides enforcement for forensic audits. Agencies self-audit and provide results to Congress and review board.

**What remains:** The audit is still conducted by the agency itself rather than by review board-contracted independent experts. Law 2 is actually stronger here — its Section 12H(c) mandates "Forensic auditors shall be selected by the review board and shall operate independently of the audited agency." Law 1 should adopt Law 2's independent-auditor language.

---

### 7. Compulsory Process for Private Entities Has No Timeline for Enforcement

**Problem:** Section 2(e) requires subpoenas within 15 days, but there is no private entity compliance deadline, no daily penalty ($100,000/day), and no expedited contempt proceedings.

**What remains:** Private entities have subpoenas issued but no clear enforcement timeline if they refuse. Need specific compliance deadline, escalating daily fines, and expedited contempt procedures.

---

### 8. No Provision Requiring Agencies to Disclose the Existence of Records They Claim Not to Have

**Problem:** Agencies can simply say "we don't have those records" without any verification. No "negative certification" requiring itemized declaration of record categories that logically should exist but the agency claims not to possess.

**What remains:** Need a provision requiring agencies to file sworn declarations listing every Schedule A category, what they found, and for any category where they claim no records exist, an explanation of why such records should not be expected to exist.

---

### 9. No Mechanism to Prevent Agency-to-Agency Record Transfers to Avoid Disclosure

**Problem:** No provision prevents agencies from transferring covered records to other entities (including foreign governments) to avoid disclosure obligations.

**What remains:** Need language stating: "Any transfer of covered records to any entity after September 10, 2025, does not relieve the transferring agency of its disclosure obligations." Transfers to foreign governments should be treated as destruction (15-year penalty).

---

### 10. AG Conflict of Interest Provision Is Triggered Only by Review Board Finding

**Problem:** Section 7(e) requires the review board to find DOJ personnel implicated before a Special Master is appointed. This is reactive rather than presumptive — the AG controls disclosure until the review board catches something.

**What remains:** Need presumptive recusal or automatic Special Master trigger upon enactment, given that the FBI cover-up allegations are the central thesis of the legislation.

---

### 11. No Protection Against Parallel Legal Challenges Designed to Delay

**Problem:** No expedited judicial review provision. Agencies or third parties can file constitutional challenges in favorable courts, obtain nationwide injunctions from single district judges, and delay compliance for years.

**What remains:** Need: (a) constitutional challenges heard by three-judge D.C. Circuit panel within 30 days, (b) no nationwide injunctions by single judges, (c) prospective-only injunctions that don't undo disclosures already made.

---

### 12. No Mandatory Preservation of Electronic Communications on Personal Devices

**Problem:** Section 5 preserves "covered records" but does not explicitly address personal devices, personal email, Signal, Telegram, or other encrypted messaging used by officials.

**What remains:** Need explicit extension to personal cell phones, personal email accounts, and encrypted messaging apps. Need mandatory device collection for persons of interest within 48 hours of enactment.

## LAW 2: The Charlie Kirk Files Forced Disclosure Act -- Intelligence Services

**File:** `2_US_Intel/Law_2_US_Intel.md`

### 1. Budget Reduction Penalty Is 60% Weaker Than Charter (Same as Law 1)

**Problem:** Section 5(d) specifies 10% per month with 50% cap — identical to Law 1's weak formulation.

**What remains:** Same fix needed: 25% per month with no cap.

---

### 5. No Mandatory Public Hearing Requirement

**Problem:** No provision requiring CIA, NSA, DIA, DNI directors to testify under oath in public session before congressional intelligence committees. The law relies only on written disclosures and review board authority.

**What remains:** Need mandatory public testimony requirement for agency heads, with criminal contempt for refusal.

---

### 6. DNI Coordination Role Creates Centralized Chokepoint — PARTIALLY FIXED

**What's fixed:** The law avoids giving DNI broad coordination authority to filter disclosures. The DNI is assigned specific tasks (foreign liaison requests) rather than general coordination. Individual agency disclosure remains independent.

**What remains:** Section 12B(h) still assigns DNI "personal liability for compliance across all Intelligence Community elements under the DNI's coordination authority" — which reintroduces the chokepoint concern by framing the DNI as having coordination authority over all IC compliance. Need to clarify that DNI liability is for DNI's own office compliance only, not coordination authority over other agencies' independent disclosure obligations.

---

### 9. No Provision for Intercepting Real-Time Intelligence Destruction — PARTIALLY FIXED

**What's fixed:** Section 6(f) establishes "48-Hour Emergency Response for Evidence Destruction" requiring immediate halt of all destruction upon report.

**What remains:** Prospective collection of new intelligence after enactment with 72-hour production is not explicitly mandated. Need provision requiring agencies to collect and produce any newly generated intelligence related to Schedule A items on a rolling 72-hour basis post-enactment.

---

### 7. State and Local Intelligence Cooperation Is a Single Sentence

**Problem:** Section 12 provides only minimal coverage: 30-day production with 10% grant suspension. No explicit coverage of state intelligence fusion centers or state DPS intelligence divisions.

**What remains:** Need import of enhanced Law 1 penalty structure (escalating to 25% of all federal funding) with explicit coverage of state intelligence fusion centers and state DPS intelligence divisions.

## LAW 3: The Charlie Kirk Mandatory Investigation Act

**File:** `3_Require_to_Investigate/Law_3_Require_to_Investigate.md`

### 1. Agencies Investigate Themselves -- Structural Conflict of Interest

**Problem:** Sections 2, 2A, 2B, and 3 maintain a self-investigation structure. Each agency (FBI, CIA, NSA, DNI) investigates itself. Teams remain agency employees, work within agency infrastructure.

**What remains:** No separate independent commission established. Need congressionally chartered special commission with its own staff, office space, and IT infrastructure, or at minimum, cross-agency staffing (FBI team investigates CIA, CIA team investigates FBI).

---

### 2. No Explicit Penalty for Bad-Faith Investigation

**Problem:** Section 1(c) requires "good-faith investigation" but Law 3 has no mechanism for bad-faith prosecution. Section 4(a)-(b) criminalizes directing investigators to narrow inquiries, but does not criminalize investigators conducting sham investigations.

**What remains:** Need explicit criminalization of bad-faith investigation conduct, automatic removal of team leaders upon bad-faith certification by external monitor, and connection to budget reductions.

---

### 3. No Budget Reduction Penalties for Non-Compliance with Investigation Mandates

**Problem:** Law 3 has no budget reduction penalties for agencies failing to begin investigations within 60 days, failing to staff 20-person teams, or failing to fund investigations with $10 million. Law 1 has 10% (should be 25%), but Law 3 has nothing.

**What remains:** Need parallel budget reduction mechanism in Law 3 for investigation mandate non-compliance.

---

### 4. External Monitor Is a Single Person -- Single Point of Failure

**Problem:** Section 6(a) establishes a single external monitor with no deputy. If the monitor dies or is incapacitated, no automatic successor is named. There is a 7-day congressional appointment window but no pre-confirmed deputy.

**What remains:** Need either a 5-member oversight board or a deputy monitor who automatically succeeds, with pre-confirmation by Congress.

---

### 5. Investigation Teams Can Be Starved of Resources Without Violating the Law

**Problem:** Law 3 mandates $10M and 20 agents but lacks mandatory infrastructure: no dedicated SCIF-rated office space, no independent IT systems, no secure communications requirement, no certification of adequate resources by monitor.

**What remains:** Need mandatory infrastructure provisions: independent office space, IT systems, secure comms, and monitor certification of adequate resources with remediation timeline for deficiencies.

---

### 6. 60-Day Commencement Deadline Has No Enforcement Teeth

**Problem:** Sections 2(a), 2A(a), 2B(a), 3(a) require investigations begin within 60 days but have no specific penalty for missing the deadline. No automatic contempt referral, no budget penalty tied to commencement.

**What remains:** Need specific penalties for failure to commence: automatic contempt referral, authority for monitor to constitute team from other agencies, personal prosecution for willful delay.

---

### 8. Charter Requires Four Separate Investigation Teams but Law 3 Has Them All Under Their Own Agencies

**Problem:** Teams operate within their own agencies with no anti-coordination provision. No requirement for independent facilities or prohibition on sharing draft findings.

**What remains:** Need anti-coordination provision preventing teams from sharing draft findings until public reports released. Need requirement for facilities outside host agencies and independent IT systems.

## LAW 4: The Charlie Kirk Trusted Investigators Act

**File:** `4_Trusted_Investigations/Law_4_Trusted_Investigations.md`

### 1. Named Investigators Can Be Threatened, Incapacitated, or Killed — PARTIALLY FIXED

**What's fixed:** Section 10(a)-(b): succession by remaining investigators nominating replacement with 14-day congressional confirmation. Section 10(c): acting authority through senior government team member.

**What remains:** No pre-approved successors pre-confirmed by Congress, and no dead-man's switch transferring to congressional commission if 3+ investigators incapacitated within 90 days. Need pre-confirmed deputy investigators and automatic transfer provision.

---

### 2. Security Clearance Bottleneck Can Be Weaponized

**Problem:** Section 2(c) requires clearances within 30 days but contains no interim clearance provision. No immediate TOP SECRET/SCI access pending background investigation. No three-judge panel for clearance denials.

**What remains:** Need immediate interim clearances with full access, background investigations proceeding in parallel, and judicial review of clearance denials.

---

### 3. Government Employees May Be Deterred from Volunteering Despite Legal Protections

**Problem:** Section 3(b)-(c) provides legal protections but no positive incentives. No guaranteed promotions, no protection from RIF, no financial incentives for breakthroughs.

**What remains:** Need guaranteed promotions, priority placement upon return, mandatory awards up to $1M for significant breakthroughs, and SEC-style whistleblower bounty program.

---

### 4. 34 Teams Creates Coordination Chaos and Resource Competition

**Problem:** $5M per investigator ($85M total) for 34 teams = $2.5M per team, far below Law 3's $10M per team. Only voluntary coordination through shared findings database.

**What remains:** Need increase to $5M+ per team (matching Law 3) and mandatory coordination mechanisms to prevent scheduling conflicts for witness interviews.

---

### 5. Some Named Investigators Hold Government Positions -- Constitutional Conflict

**Problem:** Section 1(a) names Joe Kent (NCTC Director) and Tulsi Gabbard (DNI) without requiring resignation or leave of absence. Creates constitutional and conflict-of-interest issues.

**What remains:** Need provision requiring government-employed investigators to resign or take formal leave before assuming investigator duties.

---

### 7. No Anti-Surveillance Provision for Investigator Communications with Sources

**Problem:** Section 9(b) prohibits surveillance of investigators and family but NOT of investigator-source communications. No criminalization of source surveillance. No agency-proof secure communications.

**What remains:** Need: unauthorized surveillance of investigator-source communications as federal crime (15 years), independent agency-proof secure communication devices.

---

### 9. No Provision for Trusted Investigators to Conduct International Investigations — PARTIALLY FIXED

**What's fixed:** Section 4A(f) requires Secretary of State to invoke MLATs, permits independent foreign government records requests with 7-day transmission. Section 4B(b) coordinates with Interpol.

**What remains:** Explicit international travel authority and diplomatic security funding are not clearly mandated. Need provision granting investigators authority to travel internationally with diplomatic security detail, funded from investigation budget, and diplomatic passport/immunity provisions for investigative travel.

## CROSS-CUTTING PROBLEMS — REMAINING UNFIXED

### C-1. All Four Laws Use 10% Budget Reduction -- Charter Requires 25%

**Problem:** All four laws (where applicable) use 10%/month with 50% cap. Charter requires 25% per month with no cap.

**What remains:** Global change needed across all four laws: 10% → 25%, remove 50% cap. Law 3 needs budget reduction provisions added entirely.

---

### C-4. No Provision Preventing Legislative Repeal or Waiver

**Problem:** No provision requires two-thirds vote for repeal. No mandatory funding mechanism prevents appropriations riders from defunding compliance. Investigation funding is discretionary, not mandatory.

**What remains:** Need: (a) two-thirds supermajority requirement for repeal/amendment, (b) mandatory (not discretionary) funding, (c) prohibition on defunding through appropriations riders.

---

### C-5. No Coordination Mechanism Between the Four Laws — PARTIALLY FIXED

**What's fixed:** Law 4 Section 12 establishes voluntary shared findings database for its 34 teams.

**What remains:** No cross-law coordination body. If all four laws pass, there would be 4 agency teams (Law 3) + 34 Trusted Investigator teams (Law 4) with no mandatory coordination. Need Joint Oversight Council with monthly meetings.

---

### C-8. No Public Dashboard or Transparency Mechanism for Compliance Tracking

**Problem:** Law 1 Section 8(b) requires monthly reports to Congress but no public dashboard. No real-time tracking beyond periodic congressional reports.

**What remains:** Need public compliance dashboard showing: each agency's compliance status, specific Schedule A items addressed/unaddressed, redaction counts, pending disputes, investigation team status. Updated in real-time.
