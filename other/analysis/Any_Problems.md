# Problems, Vulnerabilities, and Recommended Fixes -- Comprehensive Analysis

**Analyzed by:** Attorney review of all four laws against the primary charter (`other/List_Of_Laws.md`) and the full investigative record (`other/Bkup_Charlie_Kirk.txt`)
**Date:** March 19, 2026
**Method:** Threat analysis from the perspective of foreign and domestic intelligence services, compromised politicians, and agencies that want to keep the investigation covered up. Each problem is assessed for its ability to sabotage, delay, or undermine the laws' ultimate goal: forcing the full truth about the assassination of Charlie Kirk to come out.

## LAW 1: The Charlie Kirk Files Forced Disclosure Act -- Law Enforcement

**File:** `1_DoJ_FBI/Law_1_DoJ_FBI.md`

### 1. Schedule A Item Count Inconsistency Across the Law

**Problem:** The law's Purpose section references "213 specific disclosure items," and Schedule A is titled "213 Critical Disclosure Items." However, the goals file (List_Of_Laws.md) references "205 specific disclosure items" for Law 1. The actual Schedule A in the law text contains 213 items. If the count drifts between the law text and companion documents, an agency could argue ambiguity about which version of Schedule A controls.

**Solution:** Standardize the Schedule A item count across all four laws and the goals file. Use the highest count (currently 213 for Law 1, 214 for Law 2, 208 for Laws 3 and 4). Each law should reference the same unified Schedule A version, with a clear statement that the GitHub repository version as of enactment date controls.

---

### 2. Review Board Appointment Structure Vulnerable to Political Capture

**Problem:** Section 8(a) has members appointed by: Speaker of the House (1), Senate Majority Leader (1), Senate Minority Leader (1), Chief Justice (1), and Comptroller General (1). This is better than the previous all-congressional-leadership model, but 3 of 5 appointments are still by congressional leaders. If both chambers' majority leadership want to suppress the investigation (which is plausible given evidence of bipartisan intelligence community ties), they control a majority of the board.

**Solution:** Add a requirement that at least 2 of the 5 members must have no prior government service, and at least 1 must be a retired federal judge. Add a provision that any board member who fails to attend 3 consecutive meetings is automatically removed and replaced by the appointing authority within 14 days. Add a provision allowing any 3 Designated Trusted Investigators (from Law 4) to petition for removal of a board member for cause, with judicial review.

---

### 3. No Provision for Independent Exhumation and Forensic Examination

**Problem:** The investigation file contains extensive evidence that no proper autopsy was performed (Schedule A #210), that the body was removed from the hospital quickly, and that there is no known public gravesite. If the body exists, a new independent autopsy could resolve critical questions (bullet caliber, explosion evidence, wound trajectory). No law contains authority to order exhumation and independent forensic examination.

**Solution:** Add a provision in Law 1 (or Law 3) granting the review board and any Designated Trusted Investigator authority to petition a federal court for an order of exhumation and independent forensic examination of Charlie Kirk's remains, conducted by forensic pathologists selected by the review board with no connection to any covered agency. Results shall be made public.

---

### 4. No Provision Addressing Stingray/IMSI Catcher Data at UVU

**Problem:** The investigation file documents claims that a "Stingray" device was present at UVU during the event, used for mass surveillance of attendees' phones. Schedule A does not specifically mandate disclosure of law enforcement IMSI catcher deployment data. This is a critical forensic tool that could identify every phone present and map communications in real-time.

**Solution:** Add a Schedule A item requiring disclosure of all IMSI catcher/Stingray deployment records for any law enforcement or intelligence device operating within 5 miles of UVU on September 10, 2025, including all captured device identifiers, all communications metadata intercepted, and the identity of the agency that deployed the device.

---

### 5. Private Right of Action Still Has 14-Day Notice Requirement That Creates Delay

**Problem:** Section 9(a) requires 14 days' written notice before citizens can sue. While this is improved from the previously identified 60-day problem, 14 days is still a window during which an agency can destroy evidence after being put on notice. Section 9(a-1) provides an emergency injunction for imminent destruction, which partially addresses this, but the standard track still has a 14-day gap.

**Solution:** Reduce the standard notice period to 7 days, or add a provision that the notice period is automatically tolled (paused) from the moment the citizen files, requiring the agency to preserve all records immediately upon receiving notice.

---

### 6. No Provision Requiring Independent Ballistics Testing

**Problem:** All ballistics provisions require disclosure of existing FBI analyses. But if the FBI conducted a sham ballistics analysis or simply did not test critical questions (was a 9mm fired? was the microphone an explosive?), mere disclosure of existing records produces nothing useful. No law mandates that the review board or investigation teams commission their own independent ballistics, acoustic, and explosive residue testing.

**Solution:** Add a provision in Law 1 Section 8 (or Law 3) granting the review board explicit authority to commission independent forensic testing -- ballistics, acoustic analysis, explosive residue testing, metallurgical analysis -- using experts selected by the board, funded from the appropriated budget. Results shall be made public within 30 days of completion.

---

### 7. Section Numbering Error -- Section 0 Followed by Section 2

**Problem:** The law jumps from Section 0 (Congressional Findings) directly to Section 2 (Broad Mandatory Disclosure), then Section 3 (Specific Mandatory Disclosures), then Schedule A, then Section 1 (Definitions). This non-sequential numbering could create confusion in legal proceedings about which section controls, and a court could potentially find the definitions section applies only to subsequent sections under standard legislative construction rules.

**Solution:** Renumber sections sequentially: Section 1 (Congressional Findings), Section 2 (Definitions), Section 3 (Broad Mandatory Disclosure), etc. Or add a construction clause stating "Section numbers are for organizational convenience only and do not affect the scope or applicability of any provision."

---

### 8. No Provision for Grand Jury Referral by the Review Board

**Problem:** The review board can refer agency heads for prosecution to DOJ (Section 14A(c)), but DOJ has an inherent conflict of interest. If DOJ declines, the board can appoint independent counsel. But there is no provision allowing the review board to directly present evidence to a federal grand jury, bypassing DOJ entirely.

**Solution:** Add a provision allowing the review board, upon a finding of probable cause that a federal crime has been committed, to present evidence directly to a federal grand jury through independent counsel retained by the board, without requiring DOJ approval or referral.

---

### 9. Mandatory Exculpatory Disclosure for Defense Has No Enforcement Specific to State Court

**Problem:** Section 12K establishes a statutory Brady obligation requiring federal agencies to disclose exculpatory evidence to Tyler Robinson's defense team within 5 days. But the mechanism for enforcing this in the state court prosecution is unclear. The Utah state court may not recognize a federal statutory obligation imposed on federal agencies, and there is no mechanism for the defense to compel federal agency compliance through the state court.

**Solution:** Add a provision establishing that any Designated Trusted Investigator or the review board may file an amicus brief or intervene in the state prosecution for the limited purpose of ensuring compliance with the Brady obligation. Add a provision that any federal agency that fails to comply with Section 12K may be cited for contempt by any federal district court upon petition by the review board or any Designated Trusted Investigator.

---

### 10. No Provision Addressing the Bomb Threat at the Closer Hospital

**Problem:** The investigation file documents that a bomb scare occurred at the closer hospital (Intermountain Health Utah Valley Regional) at the same time Charlie Kirk was shot, requiring NDAs from staff. This is a critical piece of evidence suggesting pre-planned diversion of the victim to a controlled hospital. No Schedule A item specifically addresses this bomb threat.

**Solution:** Add a Schedule A item requiring disclosure of all records related to any bomb threat, security incident, or emergency evacuation at any hospital within 20 miles of UVU on September 10, 2025, including the identity of the caller, the time of the call, all 911 dispatcher records, all hospital security records, and all NDAs signed by hospital staff related to the incident.

## LAW 2: The Charlie Kirk Files Forced Disclosure Act -- Intelligence Services

**File:** `2_US_Intel/Law_2_US_Intel.md`

### 1. Rolling 72-Hour Production Has No Independent Verification Mechanism

**Problem:** Section 2B mandates that newly generated intelligence be produced within 72 hours. But there is no independent mechanism to verify that agencies are actually flagging and producing new intelligence. An agency could simply claim no new intelligence was generated. The compliance officer (Section 2B(c)) is an agency employee with divided loyalty.

**Solution:** Require the compliance officer to be selected from a list of candidates approved by the review board. Require quarterly forensic audits of intelligence production databases to verify that no intelligence products flagged as responsive to Schedule A subjects were withheld. Grant Designated Trusted Investigators (Law 4) real-time read access to intelligence production logs.

---

### 2. No Provision Specifically Addressing XKeyscore and Similar NSA Mass Surveillance Tools

**Problem:** The investigation file specifically mentions the need for "XKeyScore access with a warrant" to identify communications between investigation subjects and intelligence services. NSA's mass surveillance capabilities (XKeyscore, PRISM, etc.) are the most powerful tools available to identify foreign intelligence involvement, but the law does not specifically mandate querying these systems for investigation-related subjects.

**Solution:** Add a provision requiring the NSA to conduct XKeyscore queries (and queries of all comparable mass surveillance tools) for all persons listed in Schedule B, all phone numbers and device identifiers listed in Schedule A, and all aircraft tail numbers, and to produce all results within 30 days. This should be a specific, mandatory query requirement, not merely a disclosure of existing reports.

---

### 3. Satellite Imagery Item (#210) Does Not Cover the Day of the Assassination in Sufficient Detail

**Problem:** Schedule A Item #210 requires satellite imagery of UVU campus for September 9-11, but does not specify the temporal resolution needed. A single daily pass would miss the assassination itself. The NRO can task satellites for near-real-time collection, and imagery from the actual moment of the shooting could definitively establish shooter location(s).

**Solution:** Amend Item #210 to require all satellite imagery of UVU campus with timestamps between 11:00 AM and 1:00 PM MT on September 10, 2025, at the highest available resolution and temporal frequency. Require disclosure of whether any tasking order was placed for satellite coverage of UVU on that date, and if so, by whom.

---

### 4. Review Board Appointment Structure Is Weaker Than Law 1

**Problem:** Section 9(a) has review board members appointed 2 by Speaker, 2 by Senate Majority Leader, 1 by Senate Minority Leader. This gives the majority party complete control (4 of 5 appointments). Law 1's structure (which includes Chief Justice and Comptroller General appointments) is superior.

**Solution:** Align Law 2's review board appointment structure with Law 1's: 1 by Speaker, 1 by Senate Majority Leader, 1 by Senate Minority Leader, 1 by Chief Justice, 1 by Comptroller General. This provides genuine independence from partisan control.

---

### 5. No Provision for Requiring NSA to Produce Communications Intercepts from Foreign Countries

**Problem:** The NSA routinely intercepts communications of foreign intelligence services (Mossad, Egyptian GID, DGSE). If any of these services discussed or planned the assassination, NSA likely has intercepts. The law requires disclosure of existing intelligence products but does not mandate the NSA to conduct targeted queries of its foreign intercept databases.

**Solution:** Add a provision requiring the NSA to conduct targeted queries of its SIGINT databases for any communication mentioning Charlie Kirk, TPUSA, Utah Valley University, or any person listed in Schedule B, by any foreign intelligence service or foreign government official, for the period January 1, 2025 through December 31, 2025. All results shall be produced within 30 days.

---

### 6. Private Right of Action Notice Period is 60 Days -- Far Too Long

**Problem:** Section 10(a) requires 60 days' written notice before citizens can sue for non-compliance. This is four times longer than Law 1's 14-day period, and creates an enormous window for evidence destruction. There is no emergency injunction provision equivalent to Law 1's Section 9(a-1).

**Solution:** Reduce to 14 days to match Law 1. Add an emergency injunction provision identical to Law 1 Section 9(a-1) allowing immediate filing when evidence destruction is imminent.

---

### 7. State and Local Intelligence Cooperation Penalty Is Weak

**Problem:** Section 12A references "automatic suspension of 25% of ALL federal funding" per month, which is stronger than the previous version. However, it does not explicitly include individual criminal liability for state/local officials who obstruct, unlike Law 1 Section 12(a).

**Solution:** Add individual criminal liability provisions for state/local officials who willfully obstruct disclosure, mirroring Law 1 Section 12(a): up to 5 years imprisonment and $250,000 fine per offense. State sovereign immunity and qualified immunity shall not be defenses.

## LAW 3: The Charlie Kirk Mandatory Investigation Act

**File:** `3_Require_to_Investigate/Law_3_Require_to_Investigate.md`

### 1. Cross-Agency Staffing Provision (Section 3B) Is Good But Has Implementation Gaps

**Problem:** Section 3B establishes cross-agency investigation (FBI investigates CIA, CIA investigates FBI, etc.) and Independent Commission Elements. This is a major improvement. However, the provision does not address what happens when the cross-agency team's host agency controls access to the files of the agency being investigated. For example, the FBI team investigating CIA must access CIA systems, but CIA controls those systems.

**Solution:** Add a provision requiring each agency under investigation to provide the investigating team with unrestricted, direct access to all agency systems, databases, and files -- not filtered through the agency's own IT staff. Require that the investigating team's IT specialists (from the Independent Commission Element) be granted root/administrator-level access to all relevant databases. Non-compliance with access provisions triggers the automatic budget reduction penalties.

---

### 2. No Budget Reduction Penalties for Non-Compliance with Investigation Mandates

**Problem:** Law 3 has criminal penalties (Section 7D) but no automatic budget reduction mechanism parallel to Laws 1 and 2. An agency can stall investigation commencement, understaff teams, or fail to fund them without facing automatic financial consequences.

**Solution:** Add a Section 7D-1 establishing automatic budget reductions of 25% per month of non-compliance for any agency that: (a) fails to commence investigation within 60 days, (b) fails to staff a 20-person team, (c) fails to provide $10M independent budget, or (d) fails to produce 6-month public reports on time. Reductions should be automatic and self-executing upon certification by the external oversight board.

---

### 3. No Explicit Provision for Investigation Teams to Order Independent Forensic Testing

**Problem:** Investigation teams can "conduct or commission all relevant forensic analyses" (Section 2(b)), but there is no explicit provision granting them authority to order independent testing at facilities outside the host agency. The FBI lab has been criticized for bias in prior high-profile cases. Teams need authority to use independent, non-government forensic labs.

**Solution:** Add explicit authority for each investigation team to commission forensic testing at any accredited laboratory of its choosing, including private labs, university labs, and foreign forensic facilities. Fund this from the $10M independent budget. Results from independent labs shall carry equal evidentiary weight with FBI lab results.

---

### 4. External Monitor Is a 5-Member Oversight Board But Has No Legal Counsel Budget

**Problem:** Section 6 establishes the external independent oversight board but does not specify a budget for the board to retain its own legal counsel for enforcement actions. If the board needs to file contempt proceedings or seek judicial enforcement, it needs lawyers.

**Solution:** Appropriate a specific budget ($5M minimum) for the external oversight board, including funds for independent legal counsel, investigators, forensic experts, and administrative staff. The board's budget shall be independent of any covered agency.

---

### 5. 6-Month Report Cycle Is Too Slow for Evidence Destruction Prevention

**Problem:** Section 2(b) (and parallel sections) require public disclosure reports every 6 months. This means critical findings could be suppressed for up to 6 months. Evidence can be destroyed in hours.

**Solution:** Add a provision for emergency interim reports: whenever any investigation team discovers evidence of active evidence destruction, witness intimidation, or obstruction, the team shall publish an emergency report within 72 hours. Add monthly progress updates (not full reports) to the external oversight board, with summaries made public.

---

### 6. No Provision for Protecting Robinson Defense Team Access to Investigation Findings

**Problem:** Law 3's investigation teams will discover exculpatory evidence relevant to Tyler Robinson's defense. But there is no explicit provision ensuring that Robinson's defense team has access to investigation team findings on a rolling basis. The Brady provision is in Law 1 but not duplicated in Law 3.

**Solution:** Add a statutory Brady obligation in Law 3 requiring each investigation team to designate a Brady compliance officer who reviews all findings for exculpatory, impeachment, or mitigating material and produces it to Robinson's defense counsel within 5 days of discovery.

---

### 7. Schedule A Count Is 208 While Laws 1 and 2 Have 213 and 214

**Problem:** Law 3 references "208 Critical Disclosure Items" while Law 1 has 213 and Law 2 has 214. This means Law 3 investigation teams are not mandated to investigate the additional items that only appear in Laws 1 and 2 (Items #209-#214 including Israeli IP surveillance, autopsy failure, Corner-Shot weapon analysis, state official communications, and hospital compromise).

**Solution:** Synchronize all four laws to use the same, highest-count Schedule A. Law 3 should reference all 214 items (or whatever the unified highest count is). Add the missing items to Law 3's Schedule A.

---

### 8. No Authority for Investigation Teams to Compel Testimony of Foreign Nationals

**Problem:** If Egyptian military contractors, Israeli intelligence operatives, or French legionnaires are in the United States, no provision grants investigation teams authority to compel their testimony through subpoena or other process. These are critical witnesses.

**Solution:** Add a provision granting investigation teams authority to issue subpoenas to any person present in the United States, regardless of citizenship or diplomatic status, for testimony and document production related to the investigation. For persons with diplomatic immunity, require the Secretary of State to request waiver of immunity within 14 days. If immunity is not waived, the refusal shall be publicly reported.

## LAW 4: The Charlie Kirk Trusted Investigators Act

**File:** `4_Trusted_Investigations/Law_4_Trusted_Investigations.md`

### 1. 17 Designated Trusted Investigators Creates Coordination Overload

**Problem:** The goals file lists 17 investigators, each with 2 teams (34 total teams). At $5M per investigator ($85M total), each team gets roughly $2.5M -- far below Law 3's $10M per team. More critically, 34 teams competing for the same witnesses, documents, and agency cooperation will create scheduling conflicts, duplicative work, and opportunities for agencies to play teams against each other.

**Solution:** Increase the per-investigator budget to at least $10M ($170M total) to match Law 3's team funding. Add a mandatory coordination mechanism: a shared scheduling system for witness interviews (no witness interviewed by more than 3 teams without the witness's consent), a shared evidence database where all findings are posted within 72 hours, and monthly coordination meetings chaired by the senior-most Designated Trusted Investigator.

---

### 2. Named Investigators Include the Author of the Laws (Bryan Starbuck)

**Problem:** Section 1(a)(16) names Bryan Starbuck as a Designated Trusted Investigator. Bryan Starbuck is also the author of all four laws. While this is not legally disqualifying, it creates a political vulnerability: opponents will argue the laws were written for personal benefit, undermining legislative credibility and providing a rhetorical attack vector for opponents seeking to defeat or discredit the legislation.

**Solution:** This is a political judgment call. One option is to remove the author from the named investigator list and rely on the author's contributions through the public submission process. Another option is to add a disclosure provision stating that the author recuses from any investigation of their own conduct or interests. The political risk should be weighed against the author's unique knowledge of the investigation.

---

### 3. No Dead-Man's Switch if Multiple Investigators Are Incapacitated Simultaneously

**Problem:** Section 10 provides for succession when individual investigators are unable to serve. But if a coordinated attack incapacitates 3 or more investigators within 90 days (which is precisely the threat model for this case -- foreign intelligence assassination of investigators), the succession mechanism becomes overwhelmed and the investigation grinds to a halt.

**Solution:** Add a provision: "If 3 or more Designated Trusted Investigators are killed, incapacitated, or rendered unable to serve within any 90-day period, all investigation authority automatically transfers to a congressional special committee with all powers of the Designated Trusted Investigators, funded at $50M, and all investigation team members continue under the committee's direction. This transfer is automatic and requires no vote."

---

### 4. No Provision for Physical Security of Investigation Records

**Problem:** Designated Trusted Investigators will accumulate massive amounts of classified and sensitive evidence. No provision addresses how these records are physically secured, who controls the servers, or what happens if records are stolen, hacked, or destroyed.

**Solution:** Add a provision requiring each Designated Trusted Investigator to maintain investigation records in a SCIF-rated facility independent of any covered agency. The review board shall provide IT security support. All records shall be backed up to at least two geographically separated secure locations. Unauthorized access to investigation records shall be punishable by up to 15 years imprisonment.

---

### 5. International Travel Authority (Section 4A(f-1)) Lacks Provision for Hostile Country Operations

**Problem:** Section 4A(f-1) provides diplomatic passports and security details for international travel. But if investigators need to travel to countries whose intelligence services are suspects (Israel, Egypt, France), those countries could detain, surveil, or harm investigators despite diplomatic status.

**Solution:** Add a provision that before any Designated Trusted Investigator travels to a country whose intelligence service is a subject of investigation, the Secretary of State shall obtain written assurances of investigator safety. If such assurances cannot be obtained, the investigator may conduct investigations through diplomatic channels, remote interviews, or third-country meetings. Add a provision that any harm to a Designated Trusted Investigator during international travel shall trigger automatic sanctions against the responsible country.

---

### 6. Whistleblower Bounty Program ($100K-$1M) May Not Be Sufficient for Intelligence Operatives

**Problem:** Section 3(e)(4) establishes a bounty program with awards of 10-30% of fines imposed, minimum $100K. But intelligence operatives who blow the whistle face career destruction, potential prosecution by their own agency, and threats to their families. The financial incentive must be proportional to the personal risk.

**Solution:** Increase the minimum bounty to $500K for any person who provides information identifying a foreign intelligence service's involvement. Add a provision for witness protection (including relocation and new identity) for any whistleblower who faces credible threats. Fund this from the investigation budget.

---

### 7. No Provision Addressing Potential Compromise of Defense Counsel

**Problem:** The investigation file raises questions about Tyler Robinson's defense counsel -- whether they were effectively selected by the prosecution, and whether Robinson's family rejected offers of better representation. If defense counsel is compromised, the entire criminal case becomes a mechanism for cover-up rather than truth-seeking. No law addresses this.

**Solution:** Add a provision requiring investigation teams to assess whether Tyler Robinson's defense counsel is operating independently and in Robinson's best interest. If any investigation team finds evidence that defense counsel was selected, influenced, or pressured by any entity with an interest in the prosecution's outcome, the finding shall be reported to the court and to the review board, and the review board may petition the court for appointment of independent counsel for Robinson.

---

### 8. No Provision for Investigating the Gag Order Itself

**Problem:** The investigation file documents that Judge Graf issued a sweeping gag order on his own motion (no party requested it), silencing all witnesses, attorneys, and media. This gag order is itself potential evidence of cover-up. Law 4 overrides gag orders for compliance purposes (via federal supremacy) but does not mandate investigation into whether the gag order was influenced by intelligence services or federal agencies.

**Solution:** Law 3's Section 3A already includes "Gag Order and Judicial Influence" as a mandatory investigation category. Ensure Law 4 cross-references this and grants Designated Trusted Investigators authority to investigate judicial orders and communications between judges and federal agencies.

## CROSS-CUTTING PROBLEMS

### C-1. Schedule A Item Count Inconsistency Across All Four Laws

**Problem:** Law 1 has 213 items, Law 2 has 214 items, Laws 3 and 4 have 208 items. The goals file says 205 for Law 1. This inconsistency means different investigation teams are investigating different scopes. Critical items in Laws 1-2 (Israeli IP surveillance #209, autopsy failure #210, Corner-Shot analysis #211, state official communications #212-213, hospital compromise #213-214) are NOT in Laws 3-4.

**Solution:** Unify all four laws to use the same Schedule A with the highest item count (214). Each law should reference the identical Schedule A. Any law-specific items (e.g., MASINT/satellite imagery items unique to Law 2) should be added to the unified Schedule A.

---

### C-2. No Provision Preventing Legislative Repeal or Defunding Through Appropriations Riders

**Problem:** Congress could repeal or defund these laws with a simple majority vote or an appropriations rider. Intelligence services could lobby for a rider buried in a 1,000-page spending bill that strips funding from the review board or investigation teams.

**Solution:** Add a provision in all four laws requiring a two-thirds supermajority vote in both chambers for any repeal, amendment, or defunding. Change investigation funding from discretionary to mandatory (automatic appropriation like Social Security, not subject to annual appropriations process). Prohibit appropriations riders that would reduce, restrict, or condition funding for compliance or investigation.

---

### C-3. No Joint Oversight Council Across the Four Laws

**Problem:** If all four laws pass, there will be: 1 review board (Law 1), 1 review board (Law 2), 1 external oversight board (Law 3), and 17 Designated Trusted Investigators with 34 teams (Law 4). That is approximately 40 independent investigation/oversight bodies with no mandatory coordination mechanism. Agencies can exploit the lack of coordination by providing different records to different bodies, creating confusion about what has and has not been disclosed.

**Solution:** Establish a Joint Oversight Council consisting of 1 representative from each review board, the external oversight board chair, and 3 elected representatives from the Designated Trusted Investigators. The Council shall meet monthly to: (a) share compliance findings, (b) identify gaps in disclosure across agencies, (c) coordinate witness scheduling, (d) maintain a unified public disclosure database. Fund the Council from existing budgets.

---

### C-4. Evidence Destruction Is the Greatest Threat and Current Provisions May Be Insufficient

**Problem:** Evidence destruction is the single greatest threat to these laws accomplishing their goals. The investigation file documents: crime scene paved over, witness videos deleted remotely, FBI requesting video deletion, Google searches deleted, AES factory explosion potentially destroying evidence. Current provisions include: 15-year criminal penalty, adverse inference, preservation triggered at bill introduction, forensic audits. But none of these can un-destroy evidence.

**Solution:** Add the following additional anti-destruction provisions across all four laws: (a) Within 48 hours of enactment, the review board shall issue preservation orders directly to all private entities named in Schedule A (not just through agencies), (b) Create a "rapid response forensic team" funded at $5M that can be deployed within 24 hours to any location where evidence destruction is suspected, (c) Add a provision that evidence destruction by any person creates a legal presumption that the destroyed evidence proved foreign intelligence involvement in the assassination -- this shifts the burden of proof and creates a powerful deterrent, (d) Require all covered agencies to provide write-protected mirror copies of all databases to the review board within 14 days, so that subsequent deletions can be detected.

---

### C-5. No Provision Addressing Threats Against Citizen Investigators

**Problem:** The investigation file documents threats against Candace Owens (assassination plot involving French and Israeli operatives), threats against Tucker Carlson and Ian Carroll immediately after the assassination, and Mike Huckabee's veiled threat to Candace ("they'll make you disappear"). Citizen investigators like those named in Law 4 face real physical danger. The laws provide whistleblower protections and anti-retaliation provisions, but no physical security provisions for non-government investigators.

**Solution:** Add a provision in Law 4 establishing a security protection fund ($10M) for Designated Trusted Investigators and their families. Require the U.S. Marshals Service to provide protective details upon request by any Designated Trusted Investigator. Criminalize threats against Designated Trusted Investigators with the same penalties as threats against federal judges (18 U.S.C. 115 -- up to 10 years imprisonment for threats, up to 20 years for assault).

---

### C-6. No Provision for Public Reward for Information

**Problem:** Critical information is held by private citizens, foreign nationals, and intelligence operatives who have no obligation to come forward. The whistleblower bounty in Law 4 applies only to government employees and those with government relationships. Ordinary citizens with critical information (e.g., airport workers who saw Egyptian contractors, hotel staff, rental car employees) have no financial incentive to come forward and face potential retaliation.

**Solution:** Establish a public reward fund ($25M) for any person who provides information leading to the identification of any perpetrator or participant in the assassination beyond Tyler Robinson. The reward shall be administered by the review board. Payments shall be made from the fund upon certification by any Designated Trusted Investigator that the information was material.

---

### C-7. All Four Laws Lack Provisions Addressing the Carbyne 911 System

**Problem:** The investigation file documents that Carbyne -- an Israeli intelligence-linked 911 technology company co-founded by Ehud Barak with Epstein-connected funding -- was deployed across Utah by 2023. This system potentially gives Israeli intelligence access to real-time 911 calls, location data, and first responder communications in the Provo/Orem area. No Schedule A item addresses Carbyne system data.

**Solution:** Add a Schedule A item requiring disclosure of all records from the Carbyne 911 system (or any equivalent advanced 911 platform) for the Provo/Orem area on September 10, 2025, including all 911 calls, location pings, audio recordings, data transmissions to any foreign server, and the identity of any foreign entity with access to the system's data.

---

### C-8. No Public Compliance Dashboard

**Problem:** All four laws require reports to Congress and the review board, but no law mandates a real-time public dashboard showing compliance status. Without public visibility, agencies can delay quietly while the public has no way to track which agencies are complying and which are obstructing.

**Solution:** Add a provision requiring the review board to maintain a publicly accessible website with a real-time compliance dashboard showing: (a) each covered agency's compliance status by Schedule A item, (b) the number of records produced vs. withheld, (c) pending disputes, (d) budget reduction status, (e) investigation team formation status, (f) upcoming deadlines and milestones. Update at least weekly.

---

### C-9. No Provision Addressing the Jerusalem Post Early Death Announcement

**Problem:** The investigation file documents that the Jerusalem Post announced Charlie Kirk's death before he was pronounced dead at the hospital. This is critical evidence of foreknowledge. Schedule A #27 (media with early information) partially addresses this, but there is no specific provision requiring investigation of how the Jerusalem Post obtained this information, who provided it, and through what communication channels.

**Solution:** Add a Schedule A item specifically requiring: (a) all records from the Jerusalem Post regarding the source of their early report, (b) all communications between any U.S. or foreign government entity and any media outlet regarding Charlie Kirk's death in the first 2 hours after the shooting, (c) investigation of whether any intelligence service provided advance notification to any media outlet.

### C-10. No Provision for Mandatory Body Camera Requirements on Federal Agents

**Problem:** The investigation file documents that FBI agents at the scene had no body cameras, making it impossible to verify what they did at the crime scene. For future compliance with these laws, there is no provision requiring federal agents conducting searches, interviews, or evidence collection under these laws to wear body cameras.

**Solution:** Add a provision requiring all federal agents conducting any activity under these laws (searches, interviews, evidence collection, witness contact) to wear activated body cameras with audio. Footage shall be automatically uploaded to a server controlled by the review board. Deactivating a body camera during covered activities shall create an adverse inference and constitute obstruction.
