# Problems, Vulnerabilities, and Recommended Fixes for the Four Charlie Kirk Laws

**Analyzed by:** Attorney review of all four laws against the primary charter (`other/List_Of_Laws.md`)
**Date:** March 11, 2026
**Method:** Threat analysis from the perspective of foreign and U.S. intelligence services, politicians, and agency officials who want to keep the investigation covered up. Each problem is ranked by impact on the ultimate goal: forcing the full truth to come out.

---

====================

## LAW 1: The Charlie Kirk Files Forced Disclosure Act -- Law Enforcement

**File:** `1_DoJ_FBI/Law_1_DoJ_FBI.md`

### 1. Budget Reduction Penalty Is 60% Weaker Than Charter Requires

**Problem:** The charter specifies "25% automatic budget reduction per month of non-compliance." Law 1 implements only 10% per month with a 50% cap (Section 7(b)). At 25%/month, a stonewalling agency loses half its budget in 2 months -- a devastating, immediate consequence. At 10%/month with a 50% cap, an agency can stall for 5 months before hitting maximum penalty, and even then retains 50% of its budget. This is the single most exploitable gap in Law 1. Agencies know Congress rarely follows through on appropriations threats; the lower rate gives them time to lobby for relief.

**Solution:** Change Section 7(b) to 25% per month with no cap. Make the reductions automatic and self-executing upon certification of non-compliance by the review board, requiring no further congressional action.

---

### 2. Private Right of Action 60-Day Notice Period Creates a Destruction Window

**Problem:** Section 9(a) requires citizens to give 60 days' written notice before suing. Combined with the 30-day disclosure deadline, this means 90 days pass before any citizen can get a court order. Intelligence services and agencies experienced in obstruction know that 90 days is more than enough time to destroy, relocate, or reclassify records. The "preservation duty" exists on paper, but without immediate judicial enforcement, it is aspirational.

**Solution:** Reduce the notice period to 14 days. Add an emergency injunction provision allowing any citizen to seek temporary restraining orders from federal court immediately upon the 30-day deadline passing, without the notice requirement. The notice period should only apply to standard compliance actions, not to emergency preservation or production orders.

---

### 3. State and Local Cooperation Penalties Are Toothless

**Problem:** Section 12 threatens only "up to 10% of annual federal law enforcement grants" (Byrne JAG and COPS grants) for state/local non-compliance. For Utah agencies that hold critical evidence -- Utah County Sheriff, Orem PD, UVU campus police -- losing 10% of a federal grant is a minor budgetary inconvenience. Governor Spencer Cox's office or the Utah AG could easily backfill the gap. This means the agencies holding the most critical ground-level evidence (crime scene records, body camera footage, local officer notes) face essentially no penalty for stonewalling.

**Solution:** Escalate state/local penalties to match federal: 25% of ALL federal funding (not just law enforcement grants) per month of non-compliance, including highway funds, education grants, and HUD allocations. Add individual criminal liability for state/local officials who willfully withhold: same 10-year imprisonment as federal officials. Add a federal court injunction pathway specifically for state/local non-compliance with expedited scheduling.

---

### 4. Review Board Is Appointed by Congressional Leadership Who May Want a Cover-Up

**Problem:** Section 8(a) provides that 2 members are appointed by the Speaker, 2 by the Senate Majority Leader, and 1 by the Senate Minority Leader. If the leadership of either party is complicit in the cover-up -- or is pressured by intelligence services or lobbying organizations -- they can appoint board members who will conduct superficial oversight. The board has no member appointed by the public, by the victims' families, or by independent organizations. The 10-year agency employment bar is good but insufficient: political operatives with no agency employment can still be loyal to the cover-up.

**Solution:** Add at least 2 board members selected by independent bodies: one by the Chief Justice of the United States, one by the Government Accountability Office Comptroller General. Require that at least 2 members have a demonstrated track record of government accountability work (prior GAO, POGO, or Inspector General experience). Add a provision that any Designated Trusted Investigator under Law 4 may petition for removal of board members for cause.

---

### 5. No Mandatory Forensic Audit Before Agencies Certify Compliance

**Problem:** Section 5(c) requires agencies to conduct forensic audits of their own document management systems. This is self-investigation. The FBI auditing its own systems for evidence of FBI record deletion is like asking the suspect to investigate themselves. The review board has access to verify (Section 8(b)(1)), but there is no mandatory independent forensic audit before an agency can certify compliance.

**Solution:** Add a mandatory independent forensic audit conducted by the review board's contracted forensic experts -- not the agency's own IT staff -- before any agency can certify compliance under Section 2(c). The audit must cover all classified and unclassified systems, backup tapes, offline storage, and personal devices used for official business. Certifications made without a completed independent audit shall be void.

---

### 6. No Protection Against "Slow Rolling" -- Producing Records in Unusable Format

**Problem:** Section 2(a) requires records be "publicly available in a searchable and downloadable format," but does not define what constitutes a searchable format. Agencies have a well-documented history of producing millions of pages as unsearchable scanned PDFs, incorrectly OCR'd documents, or documents with metadata stripped. The Epstein Act compliance failure included exactly this kind of technical obstruction. An agency could technically "produce" 500,000 pages of image-only PDFs and claim compliance.

**Solution:** Add a specific formatting requirement: all text-based records must be produced as machine-readable, text-searchable PDFs or native electronic format with full metadata preserved. Image-only scans must be accompanied by verified OCR text. Database records must be produced in structured data formats (CSV, JSON, or XML). The review board shall have authority to reject productions that are not genuinely searchable and order re-production within 7 days.

---

### 7. Compulsory Process for Private Entities Has No Timeline for Enforcement

**Problem:** Section 2(e) requires agencies to issue subpoenas to private entities within 15 days. But there is no enforcement timeline if the private entity refuses, no expedited contempt mechanism, and no specific penalty for private entities that delay. TPUSA, Mosaic Pro Events, AES, rental car companies, hotels, and telecommunications providers could simply refuse to comply, forcing lengthy litigation. Evidence disappears during litigation.

**Solution:** Add a 15-day compliance deadline for private entities after receiving subpoenas. Private entities that fail to produce records within 15 days shall face automatic penalties of $100,000 per day of non-compliance. Add expedited contempt proceedings in federal court with mandatory 7-day scheduling. Private entity officers who authorize destruction of subpoenaed records face the same 15-year criminal penalty as government officials under Section 5(b).

---

### 8. No Provision Requiring Agencies to Disclose the Existence of Records They Claim Not to Have

**Problem:** Section 4(a)(6) bars claiming records were "lost" or "destroyed" without forensic verification, but does not require agencies to provide a complete inventory of record categories that should exist but were not found. An agency can simply never mention a category of records -- for example, FBI counterintelligence files on Israeli intelligence activity in Utah -- and no one would know those records were missing unless specifically asked. The known unknowns are less dangerous than the unknown unknowns.

**Solution:** Add a mandatory "negative certification" requirement: each agency must provide a sworn, itemized declaration of every category of records that should logically exist based on the agency's mandate and the Schedule A items but that the agency claims does not exist or cannot be located. Each negative certification must be accompanied by an explanation of why such records do not exist. The review board shall independently assess whether negative certifications are credible.

---

### 9. No Mechanism to Prevent Agency-to-Agency Record Transfers to Avoid Disclosure

**Problem:** No provision prevents one covered agency from transferring records to a non-covered agency (e.g., a foreign government, a military contractor, or a non-covered federal entity) before the disclosure deadline. Records transferred to foreign liaison partners are beyond the reach of this law. The intelligence community regularly shares materials with foreign services under Third Party Rule agreements; materials shared with Mossad, Egyptian GID, or DGSE could be claimed as "no longer in possession."

**Solution:** Add an anti-transfer provision: any transfer of covered records to any entity after September 10, 2025, does not relieve the transferring agency of its disclosure obligations. Agencies must disclose the content of transferred records regardless of where the records currently reside. Any transfer of covered records to a foreign government or international organization after the date this bill is introduced constitutes destruction under Section 5(b) and carries the same 15-year criminal penalty.

---

### 10. AG Conflict of Interest Provision Is Triggered Only by Review Board Finding

**Problem:** Section 7(e) transfers compliance authority to a court-appointed Special Master only "if the review board finds evidence that Department of Justice personnel are implicated." But the review board might not make such a finding for months, or might never make it if board members are politically compromised. Meanwhile, the AG controls DOJ compliance, creating an obvious conflict when DOJ itself is suspected of evidence destruction and witness intimidation.

**Solution:** Make the AG conflict of interest presumptive: given the allegations of FBI evidence deletion requests and crime scene destruction already documented, the AG should be recused from the outset. Alternatively, trigger the Special Master provision automatically upon enactment, with the AG recused from all compliance decisions until the review board affirmatively certifies that DOJ personnel are not implicated.

---

### 11. No Protection Against Parallel Legal Challenges Designed to Delay

**Problem:** Foreign governments, TPUSA, or implicated individuals could file suits challenging the constitutionality of the law, seeking to enjoin disclosure. A single federal judge could issue a nationwide injunction freezing all disclosures. The law has no anti-injunction provision and no expedited judicial review provision for constitutional challenges.

**Solution:** Add an expedited judicial review provision: any constitutional challenge to this Act shall be heard by a three-judge panel of the D.C. Circuit within 30 days of filing, with direct appeal to the Supreme Court. No single judge may issue a nationwide injunction against the Act. Add a provision that disclosures already made may not be un-made; any injunction may only apply prospectively to records not yet disclosed.

---

### 12. No Mandatory Preservation of Electronic Communications on Personal Devices

**Problem:** Section 5 preserves "covered records" but does not explicitly address records on personal devices (personal cell phones, personal email, encrypted messaging apps). FBI agents who requested witness video deletions likely did so from government phones, but co-conspirators communicating about the cover-up may have used personal devices, Signal, Telegram, or other encrypted channels. These records would not be captured by a standard document management system audit.

**Solution:** Explicitly extend preservation obligations to all personal devices, personal email accounts, and encrypted messaging accounts used by any employee of a covered agency for any communication related to the investigation. Require covered agencies to issue preservation notices to employees covering personal devices. Include a mandatory device collection provision for personnel identified by the review board as persons of interest.

## LAW 2: The Charlie Kirk Files Forced Disclosure Act -- Intelligence Services

**File:** `2_US_Intel/Law_2_US_Intel.md`

### 1. Budget Reduction Penalty Is 60% Weaker Than Charter (Same as Law 1)

**Problem:** Section 5(d) implements the same 10% per month (50% cap) that Law 1 uses, when the charter specifies 25% per month. Intelligence agencies have even larger budgets and more capacity to absorb 10% cuts than law enforcement agencies.

**Solution:** Same as Law 1: change to 25% per month with no cap, self-executing upon review board certification of non-compliance.

---

### 2. "Final Output Information" Definition Creates an Exploitable Loophole

**Problem:** Section 1(c) defines "final output information" as "conclusions, assessments, and analytical products derived from intelligence activities." Intelligence agencies could argue that raw signals intelligence intercepts, satellite photographs, cable traffic, and source reports are not "final output information" -- they are raw intelligence. The analytical assessment saying "Israel was involved" must be disclosed, but the underlying cable traffic proving it could be withheld as non-final. This lets agencies control the narrative by disclosing only their own conclusions while hiding the evidence.

**Solution:** Amend the definition to explicitly include raw intelligence: "Final output information means all intelligence products, including but not limited to: raw signals intelligence intercepts and transcripts, satellite imagery, cable traffic, source reports, briefing materials, intelligence assessments, analytical products, and any information from which conclusions were or could be drawn. The distinction between raw intelligence and finished intelligence shall not be used to withhold any covered information."

---

### 3. Sources and Methods Exception Could Swallow the Rule

**Problem:** Section 4(a) permits redaction of "currently active human intelligence sources" and "specific technical collection methods currently in active operational use." Intelligence agencies routinely classify virtually everything as related to sources and methods. An agency could claim that disclosing the existence of an NSA SIGINT intercept at UVU reveals a "collection method currently in active operational use" (the specific collection capability). While Section 4(c) requires substance to be disclosed, the practical reality is that classification disputes take years to resolve.

**Solution:** Add an automatic sunset: any sources/methods claim must be individually adjudicated within 14 days by the review board. If not adjudicated within 14 days, the information is automatically disclosed unredacted. Add a materiality requirement: the claimed source or method must still be actively used against a current adversary in an unrelated operation. Historical capabilities, retired programs, and capabilities already known to foreign services do not qualify. The review board shall have presumptive authority to declassify, with the burden on the agency to prove active operational use.

---

### 4. No Provision Compelling Foreign Liaison Information

**Problem:** U.S. intelligence agencies receive enormous amounts of intelligence from foreign liaison partners (Mossad, Egyptian GID, Five Eyes). Under the Third Party Rule, information received from a foreign partner cannot be shared without that partner's consent. An agency could claim it cannot disclose Israeli intelligence about the 12 cell phones at UVU because the information was received under Third Party Rule from Mossad. This could hide the most critical evidence.

**Solution:** Add an explicit Third Party Rule override: the Third Party Rule and any intelligence-sharing agreement shall not apply to covered information under this Act. Congress has the constitutional authority to compel disclosure of all information in the possession of U.S. agencies regardless of the source. Add a requirement that the DNI formally request all foreign liaison partners to authorize disclosure, with the results of such requests reported to Congress within 14 days.

---

### 5. No Mandatory Public Hearing Requirement

**Problem:** The law operates entirely through document production. No provision requires any agency head, analyst, or agent to testify publicly under oath about what they know. Written disclosures can be carefully crafted to be technically accurate while concealing the truth. Public testimony under oath, with cross-examination, is far more likely to expose inconsistencies and lies.

**Solution:** Add a mandatory public testimony provision: within 60 days of enactment, the directors of CIA, NSA, DIA, and the DNI shall testify under oath before the congressional intelligence committees in public session regarding their agencies' knowledge of: (a) any foreign intelligence service involvement in Charlie Kirk's death, (b) any prior threat information, (c) any post-event obstruction. The review board shall have authority to compel additional testimony from any agency employee.

---

### 6. DNI Coordination Role Creates Centralized Chokepoint

**Problem:** Section 2(b) assigns the DNI to "coordinate disclosure across all agencies." This creates a single point of control. A compromised or compliant DNI can use coordination authority to slow-roll, redirect, or filter disclosures. The Epstein Act relied on the AG for coordination with disastrous results.

**Solution:** Remove DNI coordination authority for compliance purposes. Each agency shall disclose independently and directly to the review board. The DNI shall report on compliance status but shall have no authority to direct, filter, or sequence other agencies' disclosures.

---

### 7. State and Local Intelligence Cooperation Is a Single Sentence

**Problem:** Section 12A is two sentences: state/local agencies "shall produce" within 30 days, and the DNI "shall notify" within 7 days. There are no penalties for state/local non-compliance, no grant funding consequences, no criminal liability for state officials. Utah state intelligence fusion centers and the Utah Department of Public Safety may hold critical records about foreign nationals in Utah.

**Solution:** Import the same penalty structure as Law 1 Section 12, enhanced per the fix recommended above (25% of all federal funding). Add explicit coverage of state intelligence fusion centers and state DPS intelligence divisions.

---

### 8. No Provision Addressing Compartmented Access Programs (SAPs/SCIFs)

**Problem:** The most sensitive intelligence -- exactly the kind that would reveal intelligence service involvement in an assassination -- is held in Special Access Programs (SAPs) with access limited to a small number of cleared individuals. The law's broad disclosure mandate may not penetrate these compartments because the existence of the SAP itself may be classified. An agency can truthfully certify it has no responsive records in its standard systems while the real records sit in a SAP.

**Solution:** Explicitly mandate that the disclosure requirement extends to all Special Access Programs, Sensitive Compartmented Information, and any other compartmented or restricted-access program. The review board's members shall receive whatever clearances are necessary to access all SAPs. Add a specific provision: any covered information held in a SAP must be identified and produced; the existence of a SAP holding covered information may not itself be classified or withheld.

---

### 9. No Provision for Intercepting Real-Time Intelligence Destruction

**Problem:** While Section 6 preserves records, intelligence agencies continuously collect and process new intelligence. If an agency intercepts a communication right now between Egyptian intelligence officers discussing the Kirk assassination, there is no mechanism requiring that new intelligence be immediately flagged and preserved for disclosure. Standard retention policies might allow it to be routinely purged.

**Solution:** Add a prospective collection and preservation mandate: any intelligence collected after enactment that relates to any person, entity, or activity described in Schedule A or Schedule B shall be immediately flagged, preserved, and produced to the review board within 72 hours of collection. This applies to SIGINT, HUMINT, IMINT, and all other collection disciplines.

## LAW 3: The Charlie Kirk Mandatory Investigation Act

**File:** `3_Require_to_Investigate/Law_3_Require_to_Investigate.md`

### 1. Agencies Investigate Themselves -- Structural Conflict of Interest

**Problem:** This is the most critical vulnerability in Law 3. The FBI team is led by an FBI senior agent investigating FBI evidence destruction. The CIA team is investigating potential CIA involvement. The NSA team is investigating NSA signals activity. Each agency's investigation team reports to... that agency's infrastructure. The "independence from leadership on investigative decisions" provision (Sections 2(c), 2A(c), 2B(c)) is a legal fiction: investigators remain FBI/CIA/NSA employees, their careers are in agency hands, their colleagues are potential suspects, and their agency controls their physical security, IT systems, and classified access. No agency in history has successfully investigated its own potential involvement in a high-profile assassination.

**Solution:** Create a completely independent investigation body: a congressionally chartered special commission with its own staff, its own office space, its own IT infrastructure, and no agency personnel in leadership roles. Investigators should be seconded from agencies but should physically work in a separate facility, report to an independent director appointed by Congress, and receive career protections equivalent to Inspector General staff. Alternatively, require that each investigation team operate from facilities outside their host agency, with IT systems administered by a different agency, and with the external monitor (Section 6) having authority to replace team leaders.

---

### 2. No Explicit Penalty for Bad-Faith Investigation

**Problem:** The law mandates "good-faith investigation" (Section 1(c)) but has no explicit penalty for conducting a bad-faith investigation. An FBI team could go through the motions for 2 years -- interview witnesses but not ask probing questions, review documents but not connect dots, investigate the easy leads and ignore the hard ones. Section 4(a)-(b) criminalizes directing investigators to narrow inquiries, but it does not criminalize investigators themselves conducting sham investigations. The monitor can "flag" neglected leads and "certify" whether investigations are good-faith, but what happens after a bad-faith certification? No penalty is specified.

**Solution:** Add explicit consequences for bad-faith investigation: upon the external monitor's certification that an investigation is not good-faith, (a) the team leader is automatically removed and replaced, (b) a Special Master is appointed to direct the investigation, (c) any team member who participated in bad-faith conduct is referred for obstruction prosecution, and (d) the agency's budget is subject to the same 25% per month reduction applicable under Laws 1 and 2. The bad-faith investigation should be treated as obstruction of justice.

---

### 3. No Budget Reduction Penalties for Non-Compliance with Investigation Mandates

**Problem:** Laws 1 and 2 have budget reduction penalties (even if too low). Law 3 has NO budget reduction penalty for an agency that simply fails to begin an investigation within 60 days, fails to staff a 20-person team, or fails to fund the investigation with $10 million. The criminal penalties in Section 4(b) apply to obstruction, but an agency head could simply claim "we are working on staffing" indefinitely. There is no automatic financial consequence.

**Solution:** Add automatic budget reductions mirroring Laws 1 and 2 (at 25% per month, per the charter): any agency that fails to fully staff and commence its investigation within 60 days shall have its discretionary budget reduced by 25% per month until compliance is achieved. Add a separate penalty: if any investigation team's 6-month report fails to address all Schedule A items, the agency's budget is reduced by an additional 10%.

---

### 4. External Monitor Is a Single Person -- Single Point of Failure

**Problem:** Section 6(a) establishes a single external monitor. One person overseeing four simultaneous investigations across the FBI, CIA, NSA, and the full IC. This is physically impossible. More dangerously, a single person can be compromised, threatened, bribed, or incapacitated. If the monitor is killed, incapacitated, or pressured to resign, there is no explicit succession mechanism and the entire oversight structure collapses.

**Solution:** Replace the single monitor with a 5-member oversight board (matching Laws 1 and 2). Alternatively, retain the monitor structure but add: (a) a deputy monitor who automatically succeeds, (b) a minimum staff of 20 professional investigators and analysts, (c) personal security funded independently, and (d) an express provision that the monitor's incapacity triggers automatic appointment of a successor within 7 days by the congressional committees.

---

### 5. Investigation Teams Can Be Starved of Resources Without Violating the Law

**Problem:** The $10 million budget per team is authorized but must still be appropriated. Even if appropriated, the host agency controls physical infrastructure: office space, secure communication channels, SCIF access, IT systems. An agency can comply with the letter of the law by assigning 20 agents and $10 million, while denying the team adequate office space, slow-walking security clearance upgrades for team-requested experts, assigning the team to a basement with no SCIF access, or ensuring the 20 agents are the agency's least competent.

**Solution:** Add a mandatory infrastructure provision: each investigation team shall be provided with dedicated SCIF-rated office space, independent IT systems, independent secure communications, and full access to all agency databases and facilities within 30 days of enactment. The external monitor shall certify that each team has adequate resources. Any deficiency flagged by the monitor shall be remedied within 7 days or trigger budget penalties. Add a provision that team leaders have authority to request specific personnel; the agency may decline a specific request but must provide an alternative of equivalent or superior qualifications, as certified by the monitor.

---

### 6. 60-Day Commencement Deadline Has No Enforcement Teeth

**Problem:** Sections 2(a), 2A(a), 2B(a), and 3(a) require investigations to begin within 60 days. But what happens if an agency simply doesn't begin? The law provides no specific penalty for missing the 60-day deadline. Section 4(b) criminalizes directing investigators to close inquiries, but it doesn't criminalize the agency head who simply never opens one.

**Solution:** Add automatic consequences for missing the 60-day deadline: (a) the agency head is referred for contempt of Congress, (b) the external monitor is authorized to constitute the team using volunteers from other agencies, (c) budget reductions begin automatically, and (d) any agency head who fails to commence an investigation within 60 days is personally subject to prosecution for willful failure to comply with a congressional mandate.

---

### 7. No Provision Preventing Agencies from Classifying Investigation Findings

**Problem:** Section 7A prohibits classification of certain information, but the investigation teams' own findings and reports could be classified by the host agency before public release. An FBI investigation team could produce a devastating report, and FBI leadership could classify it as "law enforcement sensitive" or "for official use only" before it reaches the public. Section 8(a) says reports shall be public "with only the narrow redactions permitted under" Law 2, but Section 8 doesn't have its own criminal penalty for agencies that classify investigation findings.

**Solution:** Section 7A already prohibits classification -- strengthen enforcement by adding: any agency official who classifies, marks as law enforcement sensitive, or otherwise restricts access to any investigation team report or finding commits obstruction under Section 4(b). Investigation team leaders shall have independent authority to transmit reports directly to the monitor, Congress, and the public without routing through agency leadership.

---

### 8. Charter Requires Four Separate Investigation Teams but Law 3 Has Them All Under Their Own Agencies

**Problem:** The charter states each agency (FBI, CIA, NSA, IC/DNI) must conduct its own investigation, and the law implements this. But the charter also envisions these as genuinely independent. In practice, the CIA, NSA, and IC/DNI teams operate within the intelligence community's interconnected bureaucracy. Personnel rotate between agencies. Interagency meetings are routine. There is a high risk of coordination to align findings -- agencies comparing notes and presenting a unified (potentially false) narrative rather than independently reaching conclusions.

**Solution:** Add an anti-coordination provision: the four investigation teams shall not share draft findings, working theories, or investigative plans with each other until public reports are released. Each team's findings must be independently developed. The external monitor shall verify independence. Post-publication, teams may share as they wish. This creates genuine independent verification, similar to how scientific experiments require independent replication.

---

### 9. No Provision for What Happens When an Investigation Finds Agency Involvement

**Problem:** Section 4(c) provides a "conflict of interest trigger" transferring investigation authority to a congressional commission if evidence emerges that an agency was involved. But it only applies to "that aspect" -- the agency-involvement aspect -- not the full investigation. This creates a fragmented investigation: the FBI team investigates everything except FBI involvement, which gets transferred. The practical result is that the most critical threads -- the ones implicating the agencies -- get separated from the broader investigation and potentially buried in a new commission's startup delays.

**Solution:** When the conflict-of-interest trigger fires, the ENTIRE investigation transfers to the congressional commission, not just "that aspect." The commission absorbs the existing team's work product, personnel (if non-conflicted), and budget. The commission shall begin within 14 days, not go through a prolonged establishment process.

---

## LAW 4: The Charlie Kirk Trusted Investigators Act

**File:** `4_Trusted_Investigations/Law_4_Trusted_Investigations.md`

### 1. Named Investigators Can Be Threatened, Incapacitated, or Killed to Disrupt the Investigation

**Problem:** This is the most critical vulnerability. The law names 17 specific individuals. If foreign intelligence services are willing to assassinate Charlie Kirk, they are willing to threaten, compromise, or eliminate key investigators. The succession provision (Section 10) requires remaining investigators to nominate replacements and get congressional confirmation -- a process that takes weeks at minimum. During that time, a team is leaderless. If multiple investigators are targeted simultaneously, the succession mechanism collapses.

**Solution:** Add an immediate automatic succession: each Designated Trusted Investigator shall designate two pre-approved successors within 30 days of enactment, who are pre-confirmed by Congress and can assume authority within 24 hours. Add a provision that the investigation continues with full authority even during any succession period -- the senior government team member has acting authority until the successor assumes the role (this is partially addressed in 10(c) but should be strengthened). Add a dead-man's switch: if 3 or more investigators are incapacitated within any 90-day period, the investigation authority automatically transfers to a special congressional commission.

---

### 2. Security Clearance Bottleneck Can Be Weaponized

**Problem:** Section 2(c) requires clearances "within 30 days." But the clearance process is controlled by the very agencies being investigated. The FBI runs background checks. The CIA manages SAP accesses. These agencies could find pretextual reasons to deny, delay, or restrict clearances -- discovering "derogatory information" in background checks, flagging foreign contacts, or simply slow-rolling administrative processing. A 30-day deadline with no enforcement mechanism is meaningless when the agency controls the process.

**Solution:** Add a mandatory interim clearance provision: upon enactment, all Designated Trusted Investigators and their selected team members receive immediate interim Top Secret/SCI clearances, with full access to all systems. Background investigations proceed in parallel but cannot be used to deny or revoke access until completed. Any denial of clearance must be reviewed by a three-judge panel within 7 days. No agency employee involved in the Charlie Kirk investigation may participate in clearance decisions for any Designated Trusted Investigator or team member.

---

### 3. Government Employees May Be Deterred from Volunteering Despite Legal Protections

**Problem:** Section 3(b)-(c) allows employees to volunteer confidentially and prohibits retaliation. But government employees know that legal protections for whistleblowers and volunteers are routinely circumvented. The FBI's track record on whistleblower retaliation is documented and devastating. Agents who volunteer to investigate their own agency will know their careers are effectively over regardless of what the law says. The confidentiality provision helps, but agencies have surveillance capabilities that make true confidentiality nearly impossible within their own systems.

**Solution:** Add a positive incentive structure: government employees who volunteer and serve with distinction receive guaranteed promotions, guaranteed protection from RIF, and priority placement upon return to their agency. Add a separate financial bounty: any government employee who provides information leading to significant investigative breakthroughs receives a mandatory award of up to $1 million (modeled on the SEC whistleblower bounty program). Make the application process truly external: volunteers submit applications through a congressional office, not through any agency system.

---

### 4. 34 Teams Creates Coordination Chaos and Resource Competition

**Problem:** 17 investigators times 2 teams each equals 34 teams. Each needs office space, SCIF access, IT infrastructure, security details, and cooperative agency personnel. The combined budget of $85 million spread across 34 teams is only $2.5 million per team -- far less than the $10 million per team in Law 3. This risks creating 34 under-resourced teams competing for the same witnesses, the same documents, and the same agency cooperation, while none has enough resources to conduct a thorough investigation. Agencies can exploit this by playing teams against each other, providing conflicting information, and using resource limitations as an excuse for non-cooperation.

**Solution:** Increase per-team budget to at least $5 million ($170 million total). Alternatively, reduce to 5-7 lead investigators who each get larger, better-resourced teams. Add a mandatory coordination mechanism that prevents scheduling conflicts for witness interviews and document requests. Allow but do not require teams to share resources through the coordination database (Section 12).

---

### 5. Some Named Investigators Hold Government Positions -- Constitutional Conflict

**Problem:** Joe Kent is NCTC Director. Tulsi Gabbard is DNI. Both are listed as Designated Trusted Investigators. But they also lead agencies that are covered by Laws 1-3 and are subjects of the investigation. They cannot simultaneously lead the agencies being investigated and serve as independent investigators of those agencies. This creates a constitutional separation-of-powers issue and a practical conflict of interest. Congressional opponents will use this to challenge the entire law.

**Solution:** Add a provision requiring any Designated Trusted Investigator who holds a government position to resign from that position or take a formal leave of absence before assuming investigator duties. Their government role should be filled by an acting official. Alternatively, acknowledge the conflict explicitly and state that government-employed Trusted Investigators investigate only agencies other than the one they lead.

---

### 6. No Explicit Subpoena Power for Trusted Investigators Over Private Entities

**Problem:** Section 4(a) gives teams authority to "compel production of any document or record" and access agency files. But it does not explicitly grant subpoena power over private entities -- TPUSA, Mosaic Pro Events, hotels, rental car companies, banks, telecoms. The authority to compel seems directed at government agencies. Private entities could refuse cooperation and force each team into separate litigation.

**Solution:** Add explicit subpoena power for each Designated Trusted Investigator over any private entity holding records described in Schedule A. Model on congressional subpoena power. Non-compliance with a Trusted Investigator subpoena shall be referred for contempt proceedings through the congressional committees. Add an expedited judicial enforcement mechanism: any federal court shall enforce a Trusted Investigator subpoena within 7 days of filing.

---

### 7. No Anti-Surveillance Provision for Investigator Communications with Sources

**Problem:** Section 9(b) prohibits agencies from surveilling Trusted Investigators or their families. But it does not protect communications between investigators and their sources, witnesses, or whistleblowers. Intelligence agencies could monitor the communications of potential whistleblowers to identify who is talking to which investigator, then retaliate against the source rather than the investigator. This chills the most important channel of information.

**Solution:** Extend the anti-surveillance provision to cover all communications between any Designated Trusted Investigator (or team member) and any potential witness, source, or whistleblower. Make unauthorized surveillance of these communications a federal crime under Section 7 (15 years imprisonment). Provide each investigation team with independent, agency-proof secure communications (e.g., devices and systems not controlled by any covered agency).

---

### 8. Budget Administered Independently but No Mechanism to Receive Funds

**Problem:** Section 13(d) says budgets "shall be administered independently of the host agency." But the practical mechanism for receiving, holding, and disbursing federal funds requires an entity with a Treasury account. Trusted Investigators are private citizens (or government officials in different roles). There is no provision establishing the fiscal infrastructure for independent budget administration.

**Solution:** Add a provision establishing each investigation team's budget as a separate Treasury account managed by the Government Accountability Office or the Congressional Budget Office. Designate the Designated Trusted Investigator as the certifying official for expenditures. Prohibit any covered agency from having any role in budget approval, disbursement, or auditing.

---

### 9. No Provision for Trusted Investigators to Conduct International Investigations

**Problem:** The investigation heavily involves foreign intelligence services (Israeli, Egyptian, French). Key witnesses and evidence may be overseas. But the law provides no mechanism for Trusted Investigators to travel internationally, issue international subpoenas, request mutual legal assistance, or compel foreign governments to cooperate. The investigation may hit a wall at the U.S. border.

**Solution:** Add a provision directing the State Department to support all international investigation activities by Designated Trusted Investigators, including: (a) issuing diplomatic notes requesting cooperation from relevant foreign governments, (b) facilitating Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty requests, (c) providing travel support and diplomatic security for international investigation travel, and (d) reporting any foreign government refusal to cooperate to Congress within 7 days.

====================

## CROSS-CUTTING PROBLEMS (Affecting All Four Laws)

### C-1. All Four Laws Use 10% Budget Reduction -- Charter Requires 25%

**Problem:** All four laws (where applicable) use the same 10%/month with 50% cap. The charter is unambiguous: 25%. This weakened penalty was apparently changed after the charter was written and represents a significant dilution of enforcement power across the entire legislative package.

**Solution:** Harmonize all budget reduction provisions to 25% per month with no cap, as the charter requires.

---

### C-2. Evidence Destruction Window Between Bill Introduction and Enactment

**Problem:** All four laws trigger preservation at bill introduction but enforcement at enactment. The period between introduction and enactment -- which could be weeks, months, or years -- is a window where agencies know preservation is required but face no enforceable penalty. Agencies can destroy evidence during this period and claim it was routine records management that occurred before the preservation duty was "enforceable." The adverse inference provision (Section 5(b) in Law 1) helps after enactment, but the evidence is still gone.

**Solution:** Add a provision that any evidence of record destruction between introduction and enactment triggers an automatic referral to the DOJ Inspector General and the congressional judiciary committees. Add a provision that covered agencies must submit weekly preservation compliance certifications to Congress from the date of introduction. Make the 18 U.S.C. 1519 cross-reference more explicit: destruction of records after introduction is prosecutable under existing law immediately, without waiting for enactment.

---

### C-3. No Provision for International Arrest Warrants or Extradition

**Problem:** If the investigation reveals that foreign nationals -- Egyptian military contractors, Israeli intelligence officers, French operatives -- participated in the assassination, there is no mechanism to compel their return to the U.S. for prosecution or testimony. The laws focus entirely on domestic disclosure and investigation. Foreign perpetrators can remain safely abroad.

**Solution:** Add a provision directing the Attorney General to seek international arrest warrants through Interpol and to pursue extradition under applicable treaties for any foreign national identified as a participant. Add a provision suspending all intelligence-sharing agreements with any foreign government that refuses to cooperate with extradition requests related to the investigation.

---

### C-4. No Provision Preventing Legislative Repeal or Waiver

**Problem:** Once enacted, a future Congress could repeal any or all four laws. A future President could sign such a repeal. Intelligence agencies know this and could simply wait for a friendlier Congress. More subtly, a rider in an appropriations bill could quietly defund the review boards, investigation teams, or monitor.

**Solution:** Add a provision that any attempt to repeal, defund, or materially amend this Act requires a two-thirds vote of both chambers (matching the override threshold for a presidential veto). Add a provision that all investigation and oversight funding is mandatory (not discretionary) and cannot be reduced through appropriations riders. While Congress cannot bind future Congresses absolutely, these provisions create significant political barriers.

---

### C-5. No Coordination Mechanism Between the Four Laws

**Problem:** Each law establishes its own oversight body: Law 1 has a review board, Law 2 has a review board, Law 3 has an external monitor, Law 4 has 17 Designated Trusted Investigators. These bodies could produce conflicting findings, make conflicting demands on agencies, or create confusion that agencies exploit. There is no master coordination body.

**Solution:** Add a provision establishing a Joint Oversight Council comprising the chairs of the Law 1 and Law 2 review boards, the Law 3 external monitor, and 3 elected representatives from the Law 4 Designated Trusted Investigators. The council meets monthly to coordinate demands, resolve conflicts, and present a unified front to agencies. Individual bodies retain their independent authority but coordinate on shared resources and scheduling.

---

### C-6. No Mandatory Consequence for Agency Heads Who Personally Obstruct

**Problem:** While the laws criminalize individual willful withholding (10-15 years), proving "willful" obstruction by an agency head is extremely difficult. Agency heads can claim they delegated compliance to subordinates, were unaware of specific records, or relied on counsel's advice. The JFK Records Act and Epstein Act both suffered from this: no individual was ever prosecuted despite systematic non-compliance.

**Solution:** Add strict personal liability for agency heads: the head of each covered agency is personally responsible for compliance. Failure to achieve substantial compliance within 30 days creates a rebuttable presumption that the agency head willfully obstructed. The review board may refer agency heads for prosecution without requiring proof of specific knowledge of specific withheld records. Agency heads who resign before compliance is achieved remain subject to prosecution for the period of their tenure.

---

### C-7. Private Right of Action Is Too Slow for Emergency Situations

**Problem:** Across all four laws, the private right of action requires 60 days' notice (Laws 1 and 2) or similar waiting periods. If a citizen discovers that evidence is being actively destroyed, they cannot get an emergency court order for 60+ days. By then, the evidence is gone.

**Solution:** Add an emergency preservation action available to any citizen without the notice requirement. Any citizen who has reasonable basis to believe covered records are being or are about to be destroyed may file an emergency petition in any federal district court. The court shall rule within 48 hours. If the petition is granted, the court shall issue preservation orders with immediate contempt penalties for violation.

---

### C-8. No Public Dashboard or Transparency Mechanism for Compliance Tracking

**Problem:** The laws require monthly reports to Congress and public reports, but provide no mechanism for the public to track compliance in real time. Citizens cannot tell which agencies have complied, which have not, which records are outstanding, or which Schedule A items remain unaddressed. This makes the private right of action effectively useless because citizens don't know what to sue for.

**Solution:** Mandate a public compliance dashboard maintained by the review board(s), updated weekly, showing: (a) each covered agency's compliance status, (b) the specific Schedule A items addressed and unaddressed, (c) the number and nature of redactions, (d) any pending disputes, and (e) the status of each investigation team under Laws 3 and 4. The dashboard shall be hosted on a .gov website independent of any covered agency.
